The solar wind is created by the outward expansion of plasma (a collection of charged particles) from the Sun's corona (outermost atmosphere). This plasma is continually heated to the point that the Sun's gravity can't hold it down. It then travels along the Sun's magnetic field lines that extend radially outward.

Take for example an event that occurred on march 14, 1989. At 2:45 power was cut off to six million people living in eastern Canada for 9 hours. This blackout was caused by an an explosive event on the sun- coronal mass ejection- this particular event blasted billions of tons of solar wind directly at the earth. Inducing huge electrical charges, this electrical activity can damage our power distribution systems.

As these charged particles rain into the atmosphere and above the poles, they release tremendous amounts of energy as they react with the gases on earth atmosphere.

When the magnetic field is pushed and pulled by this onslaught of solar plasma, it creates huge power fluctuations that can affect power lines, overloading and damaging power transformers, tripping circuit breakers, and causing anything from flickering lights to widespread and potentially dangerous power outages.

Solar winds also interfere with television and telephone signals, airplane and ship navigation. And they can cause pipelines to corrode faster than normal.
